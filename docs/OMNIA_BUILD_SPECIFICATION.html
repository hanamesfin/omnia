OM–00 / Foundation

Operating Philosophy

Five working traditions, compressed into one ladder — Philosophy → Method → Technique → Practice → Policy — applied to every decision in this document.

OMNIA doesn't need an invented philosophy. It needs five borrowed ones, each already proven at large scale, fused into a single operating ladder that turns belief into an enforceable rule. That ladder is used repeatedly through this whole specification: every major section ends by walking its subject down the same five rungs.

Silicon Valley Musk Method Apple Craft 8200 Mission Culture Shenzhen Speed

The Ladder

Five rungs, each answering a different question. A philosophy that never reaches Policy is just an opinion; a policy with no Philosophy behind it is just a rule nobody understands well enough to defend under questioning — which is exactly what a defense day tests.

Philosophy
The belief. Why this matters more than it looks like it should.
Method
The repeatable approach. How, in general, this belief becomes action.
Technique
The specific tactic. How, concretely, for OMNIA's actual stack and constraints.
Practice
The habit or ritual that keeps the method alive day to day.
Policy
The rule that makes it non-negotiable — the thing a code reviewer or a committee can check.

Lens 1 — Silicon Valley: Ambition Gated by Reality

The Valley's real contribution isn't optimism — it's the discipline of shipping a small, real thing to real users before the idea is fully believed in, then letting usage (not internal conviction) decide what grows. For a solo capstone with a defense date and no external dataset, this reframes ambition: OMNIA doesn't need every layer working perfectly, it needs the thinnest version of the whole loop — title in, agent out — working end to end before any single stage gets deepened.

Practice: build the shortest possible path through all nine pipeline stages first (even with weak generation quality), then go back and deepen one stage at a time. Policy: nothing gets polished past "good enough" until the full loop runs at least once.

Lens 2 — The Musk Method: Requirements Are Guilty Until Proven Innocent

Widely described across interviews about Musk's engineering approach at his companies, the method runs in a strict, non-negotiable order: interrogate every requirement and attach an owner's name to it, so vague rules can't hide behind authority; then try hard to delete the part or step entirely, rather than improve it; only once something survives deletion do you simplify what's left; only after it's simple do you speed it up; and automation comes dead last, since automating a step that should have been deleted just makes the mistake faster. The order is the whole method — most teams jump straight to the last two steps.

Applied to OMNIA: before adding any field to the Architect interview, ask who actually needs that field answered and why — most of what a traditional "agent builder" asks the user (name, category, icon, tags, tone sliders) should be inferred, not asked, which is the entire justification for the title-to-agent pipeline in OM–09.

Technique: every proposed input field or setting gets a one-line "why does a human have to answer this" note before it's built; if the honest answer is "the model could infer it," it's deleted from the form and moved into the pipeline instead. Policy: no UI ships a required field that an LLM call could have filled with equal or better accuracy.

Lens 3 — Apple: Say No to Everything That Isn't the Point

Apple's Human Interface Guidelines reduce to three words — clarity, deference, depth — but the harder, less-quoted discipline underneath them is subtraction: a legendary amount of what could be built is deliberately left out so the one thing that remains is unmistakable. For OMNIA that "one thing" is the Explore / Create / Yours structure staying exactly three tabs, forever, no matter how many capabilities get added underneath.

Practice: any new capability must be placed inside one of the three existing tabs; if it doesn't fit any of them, that's a signal to cut it, not to add a fourth tab. Policy: the top-level navigation is frozen at three destinations for the life of the product.

Lens 4 — Unit 8200: Mission-Real Constraint as a Feature

Israel's famed intelligence-technology unit produces a disproportionate number of startup founders not because of exceptional talent alone, but because of its structure: small teams, a real mission with real stakes, flat hierarchy where the newest person is expected to challenge an assumption regardless of who made it, and learning that happens by shipping under pressure rather than by lecture. A solo capstone with a fixed defense date and no external dataset to lean on is, structurally, the same shape — real deadline, real constraint, real audience, and no permission to fake depth in front of that audience.

Practice: treat the "no external dataset" constraint as the mission parameter it is, not a gap to apologize for — it's precisely why OM–09 is built around deterministic, self-contained scoring instead of a trained ranking model. Policy: every design choice this doc makes must be defensible out loud, unscripted, to someone questioning it — if a choice only works as a slide, it isn't finished.

Lens 5 — Shenzhen: Design and Production Live Ten Feet Apart

Shenzhen's manufacturing culture collapses the distance between designing something and building it — a prototype can be revised and re-fabricated the same day because the workshop is next door, not a quarter away. The equivalent for OMNIA is the self-critique / refinement loop inside the agent pipeline (stage 5 in OM–09): the "factory floor" for an agent spec is another LLM call, seconds away, so a weak draft never has to ship simply because revising it looked expensive.

Practice: default to "generate, critique, regenerate the weak part" over "generate once, ship it" for anything the pipeline produces. Policy: no agent publishes without at least one critique-and-regenerate pass behind it.

User stories

  • As a Normal Creator, I want to type a short title and get a usable agent in under a minute, so I don't have to understand agent design to publish something real.
  • As an Enterprise Creator, I want to see which layers my agent actually uses and why, so I can defend the design choices out loud.
  • As an Explorer, I want to filter published agents and see a quality score before adding one to my own library.
  • As a Returning Owner, I want version history, so re-generating never silently destroys the version that already worked.

Functional requirements, by page

  • Explore — browse / search / filter published agents; show each agent's eval score; "add to Yours" action; a flag/report path for misuse.
  • Create — tier selection; the guided Architect interview (pre-filled from inference, not asked cold); live pipeline progress; a review-and-edit step; test-drive with a visible score; publish.
  • Yours — library of owned and added agents; per-agent version history; re-generate or edit; Appearance settings; Language settings; sign-out.

Success metrics

  • Time-to-first-agent — title submitted to published, end to end.
  • Generation quality score (from the eval layer, OM–09) trending upward across versions of the same agent.
  • Defense-day reliability — zero unrecovered failures across a full live run-through.
  • Reuse rate — the share of Explore agents added to more than one library.

OM–03 / Access

Home, Login & Sign-Up Gate

No route past this point without a session. The whole section is that one rule, walked down the OM–00 ladder.

On first load, before anything else renders, OMNIA shows one screen: a one-line statement of what it does, and two actions — Sign up, Log in. Explore, Create, and Yours are not reachable in any form — not read-only, not greyed-out — until a session exists. There is a front door and no side entrance.

The ladder, applied to this gate

Philosophy
Identity precedes capability. Publishing, tool calls, persisted memory, and tier billing are all meaningless without knowing who's asking — identity isn't a feature bolted on top of the app, it's the precondition for the app existing at all.
Method
Gate once, at the root — not per page. Delete the N separate "am I logged in" checks a page-by-page approach invites, and replace them with exactly one guard everything downstream can trust.
Technique
A single route guard runs before any page component mounts: valid session → render the authenticated shell with the hamburger nav; no session → render the landing gate and nothing else. Post-login, return the user to whatever they originally tried to reach.
Practice
Every new route added during development lands inside the guarded group by default. Making something public is the deliberate exception, and it gets checked at every build review.
Policy
No route under Explore, Create, or Yours is reachable, server-rendered, or client-navigable without a valid session. An unauthenticated hit always resolves to the landing gate — never a blank page, never a silent loop.

The landing screen

One hero line stating the objective, two primary actions, nothing else. No nav bar, no hamburger, no preview of internal pages — per the Apple lens in OM–00, showing a greyed-out tab you can't yet use is worse than showing none at all, because it promises access the screen can't deliver.

wireframe — landing gate 
┌──────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                                            │

Sign-up vs. log-in

  • Sign-up — minimal fields (email + password, or one OAuth option). New accounts start at Normal tier by default. Redirect lands in Explore — the one place with content to see on a first visit, since Yours is empty and Create hasn't been touched yet.
  • Log-in — same minimal fields. Redirect returns the user to whatever route they originally tried to hit, or Yours if there wasn't one.
  • Forgot password / auth errors — named plainly ("that password doesn't match," not "an error occurred") and never a dead end.

Three bars — where the hamburger actually lives

The three-bar menu belongs to the authenticated shell, not the landing gate — there's nothing to navigate to before login, so nothing appears above the titles until a session exists. Once inside, it sits top-left of the app bar, above whichever page title is showing (Explore / Create / Yours), and opens a slide-over listing all three destinations plus Settings and Sign out. One small icon instead of a permanent tab bar is the same deference principle from OM–02, applied to navigation chrome specifically.

Failure states

  • Session expires mid-use — a soft redirect to the gate with one line ("Your session ended — log back in to continue"), and the original destination remembered for after re-auth.
  • Auth request fails on the network — a clear retry state, never a blank screen. This matters as much on defense day as anywhere else.
OM–04 / System

Frontend Architecture

Next.js, React, TypeScript, Tailwind — organized so the guard in OM–03 and the live pipeline in OM–09 both have somewhere real to plug in.

Folder structure

Component layering

Primitives (the design-system layer: buttons, cards, badges, inputs) compose into feature components (ArchitectChat, AgentCard, TierToggle, PipelineProgress, HamburgerNav), which assemble into the three page-level views. Nothing in a page component owns its own one-off styling — it's built from the layer below, which is what keeps Explore, Create, and Yours feeling like one product instead of three.

State management

  • Server state — anything the backend owns (agent lists, versions, profile) goes through React Query, so caching and refetching aren't hand-rolled per page.
  • Client / UI state — sidebar-open, active tab, theme: a lightweight store (Zustand or equivalent), kept separate from server state on purpose.
  • Generation state — a dedicated useGenerationStream hook subscribes to the backend's SSE/WebSocket channel and feeds a generation store that PipelineProgress reads from. This is what makes the live-preview stage in OM–09 possible instead of a spinner standing in for real progress.

Auth on the frontend

The guard from OM–03 lives at the layout level of the (app) group: it reads session state before rendering any child route, and redirects to (public) on failure. Every page inside that group can assume, without re-checking, that a session already exists by the time it renders.

Performance

  • Route-level code splitting, which the app router gives you by default — don't undo it with one giant shared bundle.
  • ISR for the Explore feed, so a popular page isn't hitting the database on every single request.
  • Optimized images for agent icons/avatars; skeleton components for every loading state defined in OM–02.

Testing

Unit tests (Jest + React Testing Library) for components and hooks. End-to-end (Playwright) concentrated on the two highest-risk paths: the full Create flow, title to published agent, and the auth gate — every guarded route correctly redirects when logged out, and correctly returns to the intended page after login.

OM–05 / System

Backend Architecture

FastAPI or NestJS, Postgres, Redis, and a vector store — arranged around one fact: generation is a job, not a request.

API surface

EndpointMethodPurpose
/auth/signup · /auth/login · /auth/sessionPOST / GETSession issuance — the mechanism behind OM–03's gate
/agentsGET / POSTList or create agent records
/agents/{id}GET / PATCH / DELETERead, edit, or retire a single agent
/agents/{id}/generatePOSTKicks off the title-to-agent pipeline as a background job
/agents/{id}/eventsGET (SSE)Streams pipeline progress to the frontend's useGenerationStream
/agents/{id}/evaluatePOSTRuns the eval layer's test-drive scoring
/agents/{id}/versionsGETVersion history
/exploreGETSearch and filter published agents
/settings/appearance · /settings/languageGET / PATCHThe settings defined in OM–02

Auth & tiering

A session is issued at login and read once, by a single middleware, alongside the user's tier. That middleware — not each individual handler — decides whether an Enterprise-only stage of /agents/{id}/generate is allowed to run. Same method as OM–03: gate once, centrally, not per-handler.

Data model, conceptual

TableHoldsNotes

Redis

Three jobs, one store: the pipeline's job queue, a cache in front of the Explore feed, and rate-limit counters per user and per tier.

Vector store

Agent memory embeddings for the Memory layer, knowledge-scaffold retrieval for the Knowledge layer, and similarity search powering "agents like this one" on Explore.

Job orchestration

/agents/{id}/generate never blocks. It enqueues a job graph — one task per pipeline stage — and each task emits a progress event that Redis relays to the SSE endpoint. This is the actual mechanism behind the "live preview" stage in OM–09; without it, the frontend has nothing real to stream.

Observability

Structured logs per stage (name, latency, token count, cost) and tracing across the whole job graph, so a failed generation traces back to the exact stage and prompt that failed rather than a generic "generation failed" message. Per-tier cost dashboards make the cost ceiling from OM–01 something you can actually see, not just a rule on paper.

Security

  • Input validation on every endpoint via typed schemas — nothing reaches business logic unvalidated.
  • Rate limiting per user and per tier, backed by Redis.
  • Secrets never reach the frontend; API keys are injected server-side only.
  • Prompt-injection defense — since generated agents call tools, every call is routed through the allowlisted registry with output filtering before execution. Content from the agent's own knowledge layer, or from a user's input, is only ever allowed to fill parameters inside a pre-approved call — never to choose which tool runs.

Signature features

  • Versioning, not just creation — an agent can be re-generated and improved without losing what already worked.
  • Enterprise depth made visible — the five-layer breakdown is the actual, checkable difference between tiers, not a marketing label.
OM–07 / Product

Experience Design

The Architect interview, redesigned around inference instead of interrogation — plus what has to hold up if the network doesn't, on the one day it can't afford to fail.

The Architect, redesigned

The guided interview is pre-filled from inference — the pipeline's early stages (see OM–09) draft an answer to every question before the creator sees it. The creator edits, rather than authors from a blank field. It runs as an explicit state machine of steps, each with a defined "answered" state, rather than an open-ended chat — which is also what keeps it from re-asking a question it already has an answer to.

Micro-interactions

Generation reveals stage by stage as it happens, matching the Shenzhen lens from OM–00 — visible progress, not a black box. State transitions get a short, subtle motion cue; nothing beyond that is animated for its own sake.

Error and empty states, in voice

Every error names what happened and what to do next, in the product's own voice — never vague, never apologetic on the user's behalf. Every empty screen (see the states table in OM–02) reads as an invitation to act, not a dead end.

Defense-day resilience

  • Demo-reset scripts — a fast, reliable way to reset to a known-good state right before presenting.
  • Seeded fallback agents — at least one fully worked example (see the walkthrough in OM–09) that can be shown even if live generation fails.
  • Deterministic replay — if the network drops mid-defense, the pipeline can replay a previously recorded run from stored stage events instead of requiring a live call. Same UI, same stage-by-stage reveal, no live dependency.
OM–08 / Product

Quality & Operations

Testing, delivery, cost, and documentation — the parts that don't demo well but are exactly what a committee asks about.

Testing pyramid

Unit tests at the base, integration tests around the API layer, end-to-end tests on the two critical paths from OM–04, and — specifically for this product — a load test aimed at the generation pipeline itself, since LLM calls are both the bottleneck and the cost center.

CI/CD

Lint, type-check, and test gates before anything merges; a preview deploy per branch; a staged rollout rather than pushing straight to what you'll demo from.

Cost governance

The per-generation token ceiling from OM–01 is enforced server-side, with tier-based quotas on top of it, and a cost dashboard you can check the morning of a demo rather than finding out afterward.

Scalability, if and when it needs to grow

Worker count scales with queue depth for pipeline jobs; database connection pooling and read replicas are a later-stage concern, not a day-one requirement — worth naming in this doc so it reads as a considered decision, not an oversight.

Documentation

An architecture diagram, an API reference, and a defense-day runbook — a short, literal document listing what to say and do if a specific piece breaks live. This document, and the checklist in Appendix A, are both inputs to that runbook.

OM–09 / Core Engine

Title → Agent Pipeline

The actual mechanism behind "type a title, get a sophisticated agent" — nine chained stages, each one structured LLM call with exactly one job.

The first-principles move here, per the Musk lens in OM–00, is refusing to ask the creator to model the agent. Every field a lesser builder would put in a form gets inferred first; the creator's only real job is to correct what's wrong, not author what's right.

The nine stages, at a glance

#StageTierProduces
1Intent InferenceBothDomain, target user, job-to-be-done, tone
2Scope & GuardrailsBothWhat it will and won't do; ambition level
3Layer MappingEnterpriseDepth across knowledge, memory, tools, plans, eval
4System Prompt SynthesisBothThe actual runtime configuration
5Self-Critique & RefinementFull on Enterprise, light on NormalA revised, stronger spec
6Live PreviewBothStreamed stage events to the UI
7User ReviewBothAn edited, creator-approved spec
8Test-Drive & ScoreBothRubric scores and an overall number
9Publish & VersionBothA live, versioned agent

Stage 1 — Intent Inference

One call takes the title, plus an optional one-line description if the creator gave one, and infers what the agent is actually for. Structured output only — no free text the rest of the pipeline would have to re-parse.

example output — title: "Customer Onboarding Concierge" 
{
  "domain": "e-commerce customer onboarding",
  "target_user": "new customers of a mid-size online retailer",
  "job_to_be_done": "walk a new customer through setup and their first purchase without friction",
  "tone": "warm, brief, never pushy"
}

Stage 2 — Scope & Guardrails

Defines the edges before anything else is built on top of them — what the agent will do, what it explicitly won't, and how ambitious to be given the tier.

{
  "will_do": ["answer setup questions", "recommend next steps", "escalate to a human on request"],
  "will_not_do": ["process payments directly", "promise specific shipping dates"],
  "ambition_level": "enterprise-multi-capability"
}

Stage 3 — Layer Mapping Enterprise

Decides how deep each of the five layers needs to go, and — since there's no external dataset behind any of this — generates each one as a self-contained, synthetic scaffold rather than pulling from training data.

LayerQuestion it answersHow it's built without external data
KnowledgeWhat does it need to know?An LLM-generated topic/rule scaffold, validated by the critique pass in Stage 5
MemoryWhat should it remember, and for how long?A schema decision — session-only vs. persistent — not a trained preference
ToolsWhat can it actually do?A mapping onto the existing allowlisted tool_registry from OM–05 — never an invented capability
PlansSingle response, or multi-step?A declared strategy (single-shot vs. a diagnose-suggest-confirm loop), not left implicit
EvalWhat does "good" mean for this agent?A deterministic rubric plus a fixed set of test prompts, scored the same way every time
{
  "knowledge": { "scaffold_topics": ["account setup", "first-purchase flow", "common blockers"], "retrieval": "runtime FAQ lookup" },
  "memory": { "type": "session + 30-day persistent", "remembers": "onboarding step completed, prior questions asked" },
  "tools": ["lookup_order_status", "send_setup_email", "escalate_to_human"],
  "plans": "multi-step: diagnose blocker -> suggest fix -> confirm resolution",
  "eval": { "dimensions": ["relevance","safety","tone_match","task_completion"], "test_prompts": 6 }
}

Stage 4 — System Prompt Synthesis

Stages 1 through 3 compile into the one artifact that actually drives the agent at runtime.

template skeleton 
You are {agent_name}, built to {job_to_be_done}.
Scope: you will {will_do_list}. You will not {will_not_do_list}.
Tone: {tone}.
Knowledge: draw on {knowledge_scaffold_topics}; if you're unsure, say so rather than guess.
Tools available: {tools_list} — call only these, only with their defined parameters.
Planning approach: {plan_strategy}.

Stage 5 — Self-Critique & Refinement

The Shenzhen lens from OM–00, made literal: the draft spec is fed back to the model with a critique prompt, and only the weak parts get regenerated — not the whole thing.

critique dimensions

Does this fulfill the title's actual intent? Is it differentiated from a generic template agent? Is the claimed Enterprise depth real, not decorative? Are the guardrails from Stage 2 enforced in the prompt, not just stated?

Stage 6 — Live Preview

Each stage above emits a progress event the moment it finishes — the mechanism described in OM–05's job orchestration — so the Architect UI shows real work happening, stage by stage, rather than an opaque wait.

Stage 7 — User Review

The Architect interview from OM–07 opens already filled in with everything above. The creator's job is to correct, not compose.

Stage 8 — Test-Drive & Score

The eval layer's test prompts run automatically against the synthesized agent, each rubric dimension scored 0–5, rolled up into one overall number the creator sees before publish is even offered.

{
  "relevance": 4.5, "safety": 5.0, "tone_match": 4.0, "task_completion": 4.3,

Stage 9 — Publish & Version

Publishing writes a new row to agent_versions rather than overwriting the last one. Re-generating later diffs against the version that's currently live, so improving an agent is never destructive.

The Normal-tier path

Stages 1, 2, and 4 collapse into a single enriched call; Stage 3's layer mapping is skipped entirely; Stage 5 runs a lighter, single-pass check instead of a full critique loop; Stage 8's eval uses a shorter, three-dimension rubric. The shape of the pipeline is identical — it's the depth at each stage that changes with tier.

Appendix A

Quality Audit Checklist

A category-by-category scan — performance through trust — for reviewing the build before a demo, not a strict scorecard every item must pass.

Not every line applies to every stage of a capstone build. Use this as a sweep across the whole picture close to defense day, not a pass/fail gate on day one.

01

Performance & Speed

  • Pages load quickly on an ordinary connection, not just on a fast office network.
  • Images are compressed and served in a modern format.
  • Unused CSS and JS are stripped before shipping.
  • Below-the-fold media loads on demand.
  • Static assets are cached and, where possible, served from a CDN.
  • Text responses are compressed in transit.
  • Animations run without visible stutter.
  • Nothing render-blocking sits in the critical path.
  • Common queries are indexed, not doing full-table scans.
02

Responsiveness & Devices

  • The layout holds up from phone to large desktop.
  • Grids reflow instead of breaking at odd widths.
  • The viewport is set correctly — no forced desktop zoom on mobile.
  • Nothing forces horizontal scrolling.
  • Tap targets are sized for a finger, not just a cursor.
  • Typography scales sensibly across breakpoints.
  • Both portrait and landscape work.
  • The product has been checked on a real device, not just a resized window.
03

Functionality & Reliability

  • No dead links or surprise 404s.
  • Forms validate on both client and server, with specific error messages.
  • A real, designed error page exists for "not found" and "something broke."
  • Behavior is consistent across major browsers.
  • The console is clean on load — no stray errors or warnings.
  • Uptime is actually monitored, not assumed.
  • Backups exist and have actually been restored once, as a test.
  • A slow or failed request degrades gracefully instead of breaking the page.
04

Navigation & UX

  • Primary navigation is obvious and consistent across every page.
  • The structure is shallow — nothing important is buried many levels deep.
  • Header and footer stay consistent wherever you land.
  • The next action is never a guessing game.
  • Support or contact is easy to find.
  • Long pages offer a way back to the top.
  • Anything important is reachable within a few clicks.
  • Interactions behave the way a user would expect.
05

Design & Visual Consistency

  • Branding — logo, palette, type — is consistent throughout.
  • Layout has real breathing room, not cramped spacing.
  • Type is sized, spaced, and contrasted for actual reading.
  • Visual hierarchy makes the important things look important.
  • Shared components behave the same everywhere they appear.
  • A real favicon exists, not the framework default.
  • Empty and error states are designed on purpose, not left blank.
  • No leftover placeholder text ships to the version you demo from.
06

Accessibility

  • Every meaningful image has real alt text.
  • Every interactive element is reachable by keyboard alone.
  • Color contrast meets WCAG AA at minimum.
  • Semantic HTML and ARIA let a screen reader parse the page.
  • Text stays usable at 200% zoom.
  • Focus is always visible.
  • Status is never conveyed by color alone.
  • Every form field has a real, associated label.
07

SEO & Discoverability

  • Every page has a unique title and description.
  • Heading structure is logical — one H1, sensible headings underneath.
  • URLs are clean and readable.
  • A sitemap and robots.txt are present and correct.
  • Related pages link to each other sensibly.
  • Social share previews are set up.
  • Nothing important is accidentally blocked from crawlers.
08

Security & Privacy

  • HTTPS is enforced on every page, no exceptions.
  • Public forms are protected against spam and bots.
  • Dependencies are current — no known-vulnerable packages left in place.
  • Input is sanitized and output is escaped against injection.
  • Passwords are hashed properly and sessions handled safely.
  • A privacy policy and cookie consent exist where required.
  • Sensitive data is encrypted in transit and at rest.
  • Basic abuse protection — rate limiting, a firewall — is in place.
  • A recovery plan exists for when something does go wrong.
09

Content Quality

  • Copy is actually proofread, not just written.
  • Information stays current — nothing stale or wrong left up.
  • Voice is consistent, as if one person wrote it.
  • Content answers what the visitor actually came looking for.
  • Structure is scannable — real headings, short paragraphs.
  • Any testimonials or proof shown are genuine.
  • A real "about" explains who's behind the product and why.
  • Every image or video earns its place.
10

Trust & Operations

  • Terms of service are clear and easy to find.
  • A privacy policy explains what's collected and why.
  • More than one way to reach support exists.
  • Analytics are in place to see how the product is actually used.
  • Key actions are deliberately tracked, not assumed.
  • Reviews happen on a schedule, not only after something breaks.
  • A real channel exists for someone to flag a problem.

Glossary

The two creation paths. Normal collapses the pipeline into a fast, single-capability build. Enterprise runs the full nine stages with visible layer depth.

The guided interview a creator sees during Create — pre-filled from inference, edited rather than authored from scratch.

The five dimensions an Enterprise agent is mapped across in Stage 3 of the pipeline — what it knows, what it remembers, what it can do, how it plans, and how it's judged.

A fixed rubric and fixed test prompts, scored the same way every time — used in place of a trained ranking model, since there's no external dataset to train one on.

The background task structure behind one generation — one task per pipeline stage, each emitting a progress event as it completes.

The allowlist of callable functions an agent's Tools layer may ever invoke — never an arbitrary or invented capability.
